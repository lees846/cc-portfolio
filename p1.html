Eric's Lost DS

Link to Code Sketch

ID: My partner, Eric's, lost Nintendo DS is drawn in perspective on the screen with blank game cards floating behind it. The screen color and glow change when the mouse is moved.

Description

For the Lost and Found assignment, we were assigned a partner and received from them a written description of an object they've lost in the past that has significance to them. Based on the description Eric gave me, my guess is that he lost a Nintendo DS, and I wanted to take his color and locational queues and create a composition around the object he described.

The description I provided Eric is as follows:

Carrot the Caterpillar

The object I lost was actually a creature. My brother and I had found a caterpillar and our family decided to catch it, feed it, and watch it turn into a butterfly. We set him up in a tank with some leaves, carrot stalks/greens (hence its name), and twigs, and eventually Carrot crawled up onto a twig and formed a chrysalis. We watched it change over time, but eventually the season for a butterfly to emerge had past, and Carrot did not come out. It turned out that we didn’t add enough moisture to the tank, and that Carrot dried out before he could metamorphosize, and thus he was lost :( Always remember to water your caterpillars…

The tank was fairly small and rectangular, and there was a slight taper, so the bottom was a bit smaller than the top. The walls were clear plastic and the cap on top was a bright green with repetitive vertical indents on the sides. The top of the cap had a small clear plastic door we could open to add things or take stuff out (but you can’t see it from a side view). There were a couple small twigs inside, and the diagonal one that went from the bottom corner to the opposing top corner is the one that Carrot picked for his chrysalis. It’s 2/3 of the way up the twig, and by the end of his time with us it was a soft brown, somewhat triangular (but rounded edges) on the top, and the bottom half was longer and comes to a point asymmetrically/off-center. The chrysalis is only around an inch or two long, and its suspended by a small stem of sorts above the carrot leaves. Some of the small, feathery, old green leaves have bites taken out of them from a long time ago. In the end, the cap was the brightest green, and most everything else had a hint of brown and dryness.

R.I.P. Carrot

Design Process

Before beginning this project, I read through Eric's description a couple of times in order to pull out any specific details, as well as to investigate whether or not I knew what the actual object was (he never named it). I realized that he was probably explaining a Nintendo DS, so I ran with that for my p5 sketch.

I made a couple of thumbnail sketches and decided I wanted try creating a DS loosely depicted in perspective with some game cards behind it, and this way I could use a variety of coding tools to create it. I used a lot of trial and error to get all my lines to look approximately how I wanted them, and ultimately, I was able to include most of the components I’d intended to. Last minute, I decided to make the screens change color depending on where the mouse is on the screen to make the sketch more fun and interactive.

Reflection

Eric provided me with this description of his lost object:
“It’s a flipping box, with two screens on both sides. The top one is untouchable, the lower one is touchable. Few buttons located on the lower level, there should be 4 on the left and 6 on the right. Two buttons are located at the corner of the lower level’s back. There’s a place you can put cards in on the back. It’s mainly black but the buttons are all in white.”

I noticed that he used a lot of language that referred to the object in reference to its three-dimensional form (describing things from the front and back for example) as opposed to picturing it from a single reference point as I did for mine. I found it interesting to mentally translate his description to something that can be viewed in a mostly-stagnant sketch from a singular angle. I think my prior knowledge of the item I believe he was describing allowed me to create a fairly informed depiction based on what he wrote. It’s really interesting to see how his description differed from mine in structure and content.

I think my final image would have looked a lot different if I had designed it in Illustrator as opposed to in p5, for example. The nature of using the coordinate system to code an image slows down the design process for me, and I think while this is an appropriate rendition of the description I received, I think I could still take the background further, for example. I’m really happy with the lighting effect though, and I think interaction is a better way to use code than solely creating a static image. I enjoyed jumping back into code for this project and seeing how different people describe and interpret things differently.
